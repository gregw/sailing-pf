
+ sailing yachts and dinghies are called boats in racing software
+ boats are typically associated with a single club, but a few boats may list
membership of several clubs.
+ boats are identified by sail number and name.   Names are definitely not
unique. Sail numbers are mostly unique, but there are infrequent duplications.
Such duplications can typically be resolved by either name or the club at
which the boat races.
+ Names are typically manually entered, so there can be different versions of
the name (eg a boat called TenSixty might be entered as 1060; a boat called
Azzuro and sponsored by Komatsu may be entered in a race as "Komatsu Azzuro" )
+ Sail numbers are normally entered with only a few typographical variations:
eg "AUS-1234" vs "aus 1234"
+ Boats will have 1 or more owners
+ Boats may have non owner representatives
+ Some owners/representatives may have multiple boats
+ boats will have a design and a maker.
+ there are some very common designs and some very common makers.
+ there are also some custom boats that are neither common designs nor common makers.
+ some designs are tightly controlled with little variation between boats
(often called one-designs), whilst others have significant variation within a
design.
+ some designs (e.g. J/24) can have different makers, but most designs have a
single maker.
+ some boats are well maintained, others are not, which can affect their
performance
+ some boats are well sailed, others are not, which can affect their
performance.
+ some boats are variable in both the quality of their equipment and crew
race to race.
+ clubs run races and are called the organizing authority for a race.
+ races are typically organized into series.
+ some races may be part of more than one series, although there will
typically be a primary series they are part of.
+ some races may be stand alone.
+ some races may be considered both stand alone and part of a series.
+ series are part of a season, which in Australia is typically over the end of
year: so the 24-25 season will include races from spring 2024 to winter 2025
+ boats may enter a race casually (not part of the series) or they may enter
the series of races.
+ boats may enter a sub-series (eg just spring races; just races nominated as
female helm; some other sub-series criteria)
+ races/series may offer several classifications of entry (non-spinnaker vs spinnaker,
handicap type, helm gender, crew gender, small boat, old boat, boat type, amateur boat).
A boat may choose to enter 1 or more of these classifications when they enter.
+ races may be split into divisions (e.g. div 1, div 2), typically by performance, but sometimes also by classification (typically design).
+ a boat can only be in one division, but can have multiple classifications.
+ boats are allocated a division by the race organisers.
+ results for races can be scratch and/or handicapped
+ scratch results are based purely on elapsed time
+ handicaps can be applied either in pursuit mode, time corrected factor (TCF mode) or a combination.
+ in pursuit mode, an estimation of the duration of the race is used to allocate different start times to all boats and the results are
based on the finish order.
+ in TCF mode the boats elapsed time is adjusted by multiplying it with their handicap to obtain a corrected time.  The results are based
on best corrected time order.
+ Some races are run in pursuit format (with different allocated start times), but the results are calculated on a corrected time rather
than finish order.  This compensates for an unknown or incorrectly estimated race duration.
+ handicaps can be measurement handicaps or performance handicaps (in the USA they do a combination)
+ a measurement handicap is based on detailed measurement of the boat and its sails that are put through an algorithm to determine a handicap
+ IRC is a measurement handicap with a secret algorithm, managed by the Royal Ocean Racing Club (RORC) in the UK and the Union Nationale
pour la Course au Large (UNCL) in France. It produces a single time correction factor (TCC) per certificate, which may vary for spinnaker,
non-spinnaker and sometimes short-handed sailing. IRC certificates come in two forms: Standard (owner-declared measurements) and Endorsed
(verified by an approved measurer). In Australia, endorsed certificates have historically been required for club racing, though standard
certificates are now also accepted at some clubs. IRC certificates are renewed annually; the absolute TCC value from one year should not
be directly compared to that of a different year, though values are normally similar.
+ boats may have multiple IRC certificates for different configurations.  Different certificates may be used in different races within a
series.
+ IRC also has a shorthanded (IRC SH) variant for two-handed or shorthanded sailing.
+ IRC is primarily used for offshore races
+ ORCi (ORC International) is a measurement-based handicap system managed by the Offshore Racing Congress (ORC). Unlike IRC, its Velocity
Prediction Program (VPP) algorithm is publicly known and transparent. A complete set of hull, appendage, stability, rig and sail
measurements are taken by a certified measurer and fed into the VPP to produce time allowances in seconds per nautical mile across a
range of wind speeds and angles, rather than a single number. A General Purpose Handicap (GPH) single number is also produced. This
allows races to be scored using either a simple single-number handicap or the more precise Performance Curve Scoring (PCS) method that
accounts for specific race conditions.
+ ORCclub (ORC Club) is a simplified version of ORCi that uses the same VPP but allows owner-declared measurements supported by
comparable boat data, making it less costly and onerous than ORCi. Validated certificates using accredited measurer data are also
available.
+ ORC certificates come in several variants: standard (all-purpose), non-spinnaker (NS), double-handed (DH), and windward/leeward (WL).
The NS and DH variants use different VPP predictions reflecting the different sailing configurations. WL certificates are optimised
for windward/leeward courses specifically. Club certificates use owner-declared or comparable boat measurements rather than
accredited measurer data, and are considered less authoritative than international certificates.
+ In TopYacht, ORC group names encode these variants in the caption, e.g. "ORC NS WL LO results" means ORC non-spinnaker
windward/leeward. The "LO" and "HI" suffixes indicate light/heavy wind ranges and can be ignored for handicap purposes.
+ AMS is an Australian measurement-based handicap system administered by the Yacht Racing Services Association (YRSA), and is primarily
used in Victoria. Its algorithm is not publicly disclosed. The system is recalibrated on a 4-year cycle aligned with the Racing Rules of
Sailing: the algorithm's weightings and parameters are adjusted based on fleet performance data accumulated over that period, rather than
the algorithm being fundamentally redesigned. This understanding is based on how the system has been described by those involved, rather
than published documentation.
+ AMS also has non-spinnaker (AMS NS) and two-handed (AMS 2HD) variants.
+ PHS is a performance-based handicap system used in many Australian clubs, where a boat's past performance is used to calculate its next
handicap. Essentially it is some form of sliding average with clamps for unusual performances.
+ Races can be decided by a combination of sailing skill; suitability of a particular design for the conditions; luck with unexpected
weather changes; misfortune with gear breakage or crew errors; preparation of the boat; sail selection (which is sailing skill)
+ the only information available for my data analysis is the elapsed time of the boats.  We generally do not know the course length, which
could be from 1 to many hundreds of nautical miles.
+ slow elapsed times for individual or groups of boats may be due to bad sailing, bad luck with weather, gear failure etc.
+ some boats are very reliable and frequently sail to their handicaps (make their own luck), but no boat is perfect.
+ some boats are very variable and change race to race as they have different crew on board or make unforced errors (make their own bad
luck)
+ some races can have "weather or tide gates" where all boats in front or behind a particular point will do better/worse
+ some races can have weather effects where all boats that pick one side or the other can get significantly different results to the other
boats.
+ In Australia, most race results are processed either by the SailSys or TopYacht software suites. Results are typically published online. I
believe that the entry details of the boats and the elapsed times are public data, as are any measurement-based handicaps assigned.
However, it could be argued that PHS handicaps and the actual race results could be proprietary intellectual property, so I do not wish to
use any PHS handicaps.
